RELAY · DESIGN DRAFT FOR DISCUSSION

Stage & card model

A board is fully customizable — you name your stages, order them, and pick how many. To make card actions predictable across any layout, behavior comes from three fixed ideas: the stage type, whether a work stage is AI-enabled, and who owns the card. This doc defines those, the card's sub-states, and the very few buttons that actually exist.

01 — Principle

Work is pulled, not pushed

Nobody presses a button to shove a card forward. A worker — an agent or a person — pulls the next card when they have room for it. So on the board, movement is dragging (or the agent moving its own card), assignment is ownership, and buttons only appear when a human has an actual decision to make — answer a question, or approve/reject at a gate. Everything else is quiet.

02 — Stage types

Five types

Every column is exactly one of these. This is the smallest set that still tells us which columns are gates and where agents are allowed to work. Planning and Work are twins — both active, both owned — split apart only so the board can separate shaping the approach from building it.

Queue passive · nothing runs here

A holding area. Cards wait to be pulled. Order signals priority; the next stage's worker pulls from the top when it has room.

e.g. Backlog · Next · Icebox
Work active · someone does the work

Where work happens. A Work stage is either AI-enabled or not — that's a flag on the stage, not on the card:

AI-enabled — an agent is listening; it can pull & drive cards here
H Human-only — no agent listens; a person must do it
Planning active · work-like

Where the approach gets shaped — the spec, the plan — before anything is built. It behaves exactly like Work: it has an owner, can be AI-enabled, and runs through the same sub-states and actions. It's its own category only so figuring out what to do reads separately from doing it.

e.g. Spec · Plan · Scope
Review gate · a human decides

A checkpoint. Work lands here and stops until a human approves it forward or rejects it back. The reviewer acts as a gate — reviewing does not make you the owner. This type is how we know which columns are gates.

e.g. Review · QA · Sign-off · Legal
Done complete · a holding area

A holding area like a Queue — no work happens — but it also says the work before it is complete. Cards park here, done with that phase.

e.g. Shipped · Complete · Archived · Won't do
Done can repeat. A board can have several Done stages, not only at the very end — each one parks cards (like a Queue) while signalling that the phase before it is complete. Only the final Done stage is terminal: nothing pulls out of it.
03 — Ownership

Ownership is a claim on the card

Owners live on the card, not the stage. Ownership acts like a lock: it says who's driving. An agent only touches cards it's allowed to claim — so ownership is also how a human tells the AI to keep its hands off.

UNOWNED
nobody yet

Open to be claimed. If it enters an AI-enabled stage, an agent grabs it.

OWNED BY PEOPLE

One or more people. Agents ignore it — a human is driving.

OWNED BY RELAY AI
exclusive

Special & exclusive — if AI owns a card, no one co-owns it.

THE CLAIM RULE
1An unowned card ready to move into an AI-enabled stage is auto-claimed by an agent. Relay AI becomes the sole owner and keeps pulling it through subsequent AI-enabled stages.
2The mirror: when a human pulls an unowned card into a Work stage, they automatically become the owner — which locks the agent out, so no AI touches it.
3If a card already has a human owner, agents leave it alone — someone's got it.
4Assigning Relay AI is the hand-off. You add AI as an owner the same way you assign a person — and because AI is exclusive, that clears any human owners.
5Reviews don't transfer ownership. A reviewer approves/rejects; the card keeps its owner — so a rejected card resumes with the same AI owner.
Take over lives in the owners list. When Relay AI owns a card, a button sits right next to it — press it and the agent drops the card and you become the owner.

04 — Sub-states

Where the card is, and whether it's on you

Four sub-states cover every situation. The board sorts them into two buckets so a person can tell, at a glance, what's on them versus what's just running.

Ambient
Nothing for a human right now — the AI is working, or someone's quietly on it.
Needs you
Blocked on a human — a question, a review, or a card only a person can move.
In progress ambient Actively worked by its owner — AI or a person. In a Work stage.
Ready ambient* No work happening — parked and available for the next stage to pull. Same behavior whether it's sitting in a Queue or has just finished a Work stage. *Flags needs you if only a person can pull it onward.
Needs input needs you Relay AI hit a question and paused. Waiting on an answer.
In review needs you Sitting in a Review gate, awaiting approve / reject.
A Queue is just a place to park Ready cards. A dedicated queue column (Backlog, Next) and a card that's finished a Work stage and is waiting for the next one are doing the exact same thing — no work, just signalling ready to be pulled. They follow the same rules and have no buttons. The one special case is the terminal stage: a Ready card there has nowhere to go, so it reads as Done.
05 — Actions

Buttons are only for decisions

Because movement is dragging and assignment is ownership, most states have no primary button at all. A big button appears only when a human genuinely has to decide something.

08 — Notes & open questions

Decided, and still open

Decided

Ownership is a per-card claim; AI is a special exclusive owner; assigning AI = hand-off; Take over lives in the owners list; reviews don't change ownership; and Queue / In progress / Ready have no primary button — you drag. Queue stages and the Ready sub-state are one “parked” concept; only the final stage's done is terminal.

Open — the AI→human hand-back

When an AI-owned card reaches a human-only stage, the agent can't continue. It should release the card so it reads unowned & needs you, and whoever picks it up (assigns themselves) becomes owner. Confirm that's the behavior — and how loudly we surface “available for a human” vs. a plain unowned card.

Open — the queue's real throttle

If agents auto-pull, what stops everything from starting at once? Presumably each stage's WIP limit. Worth confirming WIP is the throttle, and whether a person can pin/prioritize a card to the front of the queue.
